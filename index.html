Discrete Mathematics Exam

Module 1: 20%

1. (Sentence Transformation): Transform the following sentence into its logical equivalent using inverse, converse, and contrapositive:
**"If the weather is good and the traffic is light, we will go to the park. If we go to the park, we will have a picnic."**
2. (Truth Table): Create a truth table for the following logical expression:
**[(P ∧ Q) → R] ∨ (¬P → Q).**
3. (Naming Laws): Which law or combination of laws is applied in the following statement?
**(P ∧ Q) ∨ (¬P ∧ ¬Q) = True**
(Name the laws and explain their application in this context.)

Module 2: 20%

1. In a survey of 50 students, 28 said they like tea, 18 said they like coffee, and 10 said they like both. Let A be the set of students who like tea, and B be the set of students who like coffee.
1.1. How many students like only tea? _____________
1.2. How many students like only coffee? _____________
1.3. How many students do not like either tea or coffee? _____________
2. Let A = {x | x is a multiple of 2, and 1 ≤ x ≤ 20}, and B = {x | x is a multiple of 3, and 1 ≤ x ≤ 20}.
2.1. Find A ∩ B _____________
2.2. Find A ∪ B _____________
2.3. Find A - B _____________
3. Let the universal set be U = {1, 2, 3, …, 10}, and let C = {1, 4, 6, 8}, D = {2, 4, 5, 9}. Find the following:
3.1. C' ∩ D _____________
3.2. (C ∪ D)' _____________

Module 3: 20% (Harder Version)

1. (Proof Writing - Odd/Even): Prove that the product of two odd numbers is always odd. Show your work without using examples.
2. (Proof Structure Completion - Complex Fill-in-the-Blank): Prove that the sum of the squares of three consecutive odd numbers is even.
3. (Induction Proof - Difficult Version): Prove by induction that the sum of the squares of even numbers from 1 to 2n is equal to n(2n+1)(n+1)/3.

Rule of Inference: 3 Major Questions

1. (Multi-Step Proof with Inferences): Given the following:
  • 1) p → q
  • 2) q → r
  • 3) ¬r
Prove that ¬p is true by using inference rules.
2. (Invalid Inference - Fallacy Detection): Consider the following inference:
  • 1) p → q
  • 2) q
  • 3) Therefore, p
Is this inference valid? If not, explain why.
3. (Complex Multi-Inference Proof): Prove that r is true from the following premises:
  • 1) p → (q ∧ s)
  • 2) ¬s → t
  • 3) t → r
  • 4) p
