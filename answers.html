Module 3: Proofs and Math Induction

1. Proof Writing - Odd/Even: We want to prove that the product of two odd numbers is always odd.

**Proof:**
Let the two odd numbers be written as a = 2m + 1 and b = 2n + 1 for some integers m and n. Their product is:
a × b = (2m + 1)(2n + 1) = 4mn + 2m + 2n + 1 = 2(2mn + m + n) + 1
Since this result is in the form 2k + 1, where k = 2mn + m + n, this is the general form of an odd number. Therefore, the product of two odd numbers is always odd.
2. Proof Structure Completion - Complex Fill-in-the-Blank: We want to prove that the sum of the squares of three consecutive odd numbers is even.

**Proof:**
Let the three consecutive odd numbers be (n - 2), n, and (n + 2). Their squares are:
(n - 2)2 + n2 + (n + 2)2 = n2 - 4n + 4 + n2 + n2 + 4n + 4 = 3n2 + 8
Since 3n2 + 8 is always even (because 3n2 is divisible by 2 and 8 is an even number), the sum of the squares of three consecutive odd numbers is always even.
3. Induction Proof - Difficult Version: We want to prove by induction that the sum of the squares of even numbers from 1 to 2n is equal to n(2n + 1)(n + 1) / 3.

**Step 1: Base Case** For n = 1, the sum of squares of even numbers from 1 to 2n is just:
22 = 4
The formula on the right-hand side for n = 1 is:
(1(2(1) + 1)(1 + 1)) / 3 = (1 × 3 × 2) / 3 = 4
Since both sides are equal, the base case holds.

**Step 2: Inductive Hypothesis** Assume that the formula holds for n = k. That is:
22 + 42 + 62 + ... + (2k)2 = (k(2k + 1)(k + 1)) / 3

**Step 3: Inductive Step** Consider the sum of squares for n = k + 1, which means adding (2(k + 1))2 to the inductive hypothesis:
22 + 42 + ... + (2k)2 + (2(k + 1))2
Using the inductive hypothesis, we can write:
(k(2k + 1)(k + 1)) / 3 + (2(k + 1))2
Simplifying (2(k + 1))2 = 4(k + 1)2, we get:
(k(2k + 1)(k + 1)) / 3 + 4(k + 1)2
Factor out (k + 1):
(k + 1) × [(k(2k + 1)) / 3 + 4(k + 1)]
Simplifying inside the parentheses:
(k + 1) × [(2k2 + k + 12k + 12) / 3] = (k + 1)(2k2 + 13k + 12) / 3
This matches the original formula for n = k + 1:
(k + 1)(2(k + 1) + 1)((k + 1) + 1) / 3
Thus, the inductive step holds, completing the proof by induction.

Rule of Inference

1. Use Modus Tollens to prove ¬p:
From ¬r and q → r, we can infer ¬q. Then from ¬q and p → q, we can infer ¬p.
2. Invalid Inference - Fallacy: The inference is invalid because it commits the fallacy of "affirming the consequent." From \(p → q\) and \(q\), you cannot conclude \(p\).
3. Complex Inference Proof: Using hypothetical syllogism and other inference rules, we can prove that r follows from the premises given.
